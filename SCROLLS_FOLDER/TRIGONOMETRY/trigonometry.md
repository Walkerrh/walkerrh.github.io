# College Trigonometry Notes

## Angles and Their Measure
### Description
Angles are measured in degrees or radians. One complete revolution is \(360^\circ\) or \(2\pi\) radians.
### Formula
$$ 1 \text{ radian} = \frac{180^\circ}{\pi} $$

## Right Triangle Trigonometry
### Description
In a right triangle, the trigonometric functions are defined as ratios of the lengths of the sides.
### Formula
$$ \sin(\theta) = \frac{\text{opposite}}{\text{hypotenuse}} $$
$$ \cos(\theta) = \frac{\text{adjacent}}{\text{hypotenuse}} $$
$$ \tan(\theta) = \frac{\text{opposite}}{\text{adjacent}} $$

## Unit Circle
### Description
The unit circle is a circle of radius 1 centered at the origin of the coordinate system. It is used to define the trigonometric functions for all angles.
### Formula
$$ \sin(\theta) = y $$
$$ \cos(\theta) = x $$
$$ \tan(\theta) = \frac{y}{x} $$

## Graphs of Trigonometric Functions
### Description
The graphs of the sine, cosine, and tangent functions show periodic behavior and have specific properties like amplitude, period, and phase shift.
### Formula
$$ y = A \sin(Bx + C) + D $$
$$ y = A \cos(Bx + C) + D $$
$$ y = A \tan(Bx + C) + D $$

## Trigonometric Identities
### Description
Trigonometric identities are equations involving trigonometric functions that are true for all values of the variables involved.
### Formula
$$ \sin^2(\theta) + \cos^2(\theta) = 1 $$
$$ 1 + \tan^2(\theta) = \sec^2(\theta) $$
$$ 1 + \cot^2(\theta) = \csc^2(\theta) $$

## Inverse Trigonometric Functions
### Description
Inverse trigonometric functions allow us to find the angle that corresponds to a given trigonometric value.
### Formula
$$ \sin^{-1}(x) = \theta \Rightarrow \sin(\theta) = x $$
$$ \cos^{-1}(x) = \theta \Rightarrow \cos(\theta) = x $$
$$ \tan^{-1}(x) = \theta \Rightarrow \tan(\theta) = x $$

## Law of Sines
### Description
The Law of Sines relates the sides of a triangle to the sines of its angles.
### Formula
$$ \frac{a}{\sin(A)} = \frac{b}{\sin(B)} = \frac{c}{\sin(C)} $$

## Law of Cosines
### Description
The Law of Cosines is used to find the lengths of sides or the measures of angles in any triangle.
### Formula
$$ c^2 = a^2 + b^2 - 2ab\cos(C) $$

## Polar Coordinates
### Description
Polar coordinates represent points in the plane using a distance from the origin and an angle from the positive x-axis.
### Formula
$$ x = r \cos(\theta) $$
$$ y = r \sin(\theta) $$

## Complex Numbers in Polar Form
### Description
Complex numbers can be represented in polar form using their magnitude and angle.
### Formula
$$ z = r (\cos(\theta) + i\sin(\theta)) $$
$$ z = re^{i\theta} $$

## Trigonometric Equations
### Description
Trigonometric equations involve trigonometric functions and are solved using identities and inverse functions.
### Formula
$$ \sin(x) = a $$
$$ x = \sin^{-1}(a) + 2k\pi $$
$$ x = \pi - \sin^{-1}(a) + 2k\pi $$

## Sum and Difference Formulas
### Description
The sum and difference formulas allow the calculation of the sine, cosine, and tangent of the sum or difference of two angles.
### Formula
$$ \sin(A \pm B) = \sin(A)\cos(B) \pm \cos(A)\sin(B) $$
$$ \cos(A \pm B) = \cos(A)\cos(B) \mp \sin(A)\cos(B) $$
$$ \tan(A \pm B) = \frac{\tan(A) \pm \tan(B)}{1 \mp \tan(A)\tan(B)} $$

## Double-Angle and Half-Angle Formulas
### Description
Double-angle and half-angle formulas are used to find the trigonometric functions of double or half angles.
### Formula
$$ \sin(2\theta) = 2\sin(\theta)\cos(\theta) $$
$$ \cos(2\theta) = \cos^2(\theta) - \sin^2(\theta) $$
$$ \tan(2\theta) = \frac{2\tan(\theta)}{1 - \tan^2(\theta)} $$

$$ \sin\left(\frac{\theta}{2}\right) = \pm\sqrt{\frac{1 - \cos(\theta)}{2}} $$
$$ \cos\left(\frac{\theta}{2}\right) = \pm\sqrt{\frac{1 + \cos(\theta)}{2}} $$
$$ \tan\left(\frac{\theta}{2}\right) = \pm\sqrt{\frac{1 - \cos(\theta)}{1 + \cos(\theta)}} $$

## Product-to-Sum and Sum-to-Product Formulas
### Description
Product-to-sum and sum-to-product formulas help to convert products of sines and cosines into sums or differences.
### Formula
$$ \sin(A)\sin(B) = \frac{1}{2}[\cos(A-B) - \cos(A+B)] $$
$$ \cos(A)\cos(B) = \frac{1}{2}[\cos(A+B) + \cos(A-B)] $$
$$ \sin(A)\cos(B) = \frac{1}{2}[\sin(A+B) + \sin(A-B)] $$

$$ \sin(A) + \sin(B) = 2\sin\left(\frac{A+B}{2}\right)\cos\left(\frac{A-B}{2}\right) $$
$$ \sin(A) - \sin(B) = 2\cos\left(\frac{A+B}{2}\right)\sin\left(\frac{A-B}{2}\right) $$
$$ \cos(A) + \cos(B) = 2\cos\left(\frac{A+B}{2}\right)\cos\left(\frac{A-B}{2}\right) $$
$$ \cos(A) - \cos(B) = -2\sin\left(\frac{A+B}{2}\right)\sin\left(\frac{A-B}{2}\right) $$

## Solving Triangles
### Description
Solving triangles involves finding unknown sides and angles using trigonometric relationships.
### Formula
For right triangles:
$$ a^2 + b^2 = c^2 $$

For oblique triangles (Law of Sines and Law of Cosines):
$$ \frac{a}{\sin(A)} = \frac{b}{\sin(B)} = \frac{c}{\sin(C)} $$
$$ c^2 = a^2 + b^2 - 2ab\cos(C) $$

## Angle of Elevation and Depression
### Description
The angle of elevation is the angle between the horizontal line and the line of sight looking up. The angle of depression is the angle between the horizontal line and the line of sight looking down.
### Formula
$$ \text{Angle of Elevation} = \theta $$
$$ \text{Angle of Depression} = \theta $$

## Harmonic Motion
### Description
Harmonic motion describes the motion of oscillating objects, such as springs and pendulums, using trigonometric functions.
### Formula
$$ x(t) = A \cos(\omega t + \phi) $$
$$ x(t) = A \sin(\omega t + \phi) $$

## Area of a Triangle
### Description
The area of a triangle can be found using trigonometric functions, especially for non-right triangles.
### Formula
$$ \text{Area} = \frac{1}{2}ab \sin(C) $$
$$ \text{Area} = \frac{1}{2}bc \sin(A) $$
$$ \text{Area} = \frac{1}{2}ca \sin(B) $$

## Heron's Formula
### Description
Heron's formula calculates the area of a triangle when the lengths of all three sides are known.
### Formula
$$ s = \frac{a + b + c}{2} $$
$$ \text{Area} = \sqrt{s(s - a)(s - b)(s - c)} $$

## De Moivre's Theorem
### Description
De Moivre's theorem is used to find powers and roots of complex numbers in polar form.
### Formula
$$ [r(\cos(\theta) + i \sin(\theta))]^n = r^n (\cos(n\theta) + i \sin(n\theta)) $$

## Euler's Formula
### Description
Euler's formula relates complex exponentials to trigonometric functions.
### Formula
$$ e^{i\theta} = \cos(\theta) + i \sin(\theta) $$

## Fourier Series
### Description
A Fourier series is used to express a periodic function as a sum of sines and cosines.
### Formula
$$ f(x) = a_0 + \sum_{n=1}^{\infty} \left[ a_n \cos\left( \frac{2\pi nx}{T} \right) + b_n \sin\left( \frac{2\pi nx}{T} \right) \right] $$

## Radian Measure
### Description
Radian measure is another way of measuring angles, where the angle is defined by the length of the arc divided by the radius of the circle.
### Formula
$$ \theta = \frac{s}{r} $$
where \( s \) is the arc length and \( r \) is the radius.

## Converting Between Degrees and Radians
### Description
To convert between degrees and radians, use the conversion factor \(\pi\) radians = 180 degrees.
### Formula
$$ \text{Degrees to Radians}: \theta_{rad} = \theta_{deg} \times \frac{\pi}{180} $$
$$ \text{Radians to Degrees}: \theta_{deg} = \theta_{rad} \times \frac{180}{\pi} $$

## Circular Functions
### Description
Circular functions are the trigonometric functions defined on the unit circle.
### Formula
$$ \sin(\theta) = \sin(\theta + 2k\pi) $$
$$ \cos(\theta) = \cos(\theta + 2k\pi) $$
$$ \tan(\theta) = \tan(\theta + k\pi) $$

## Periodic Functions
### Description
A function is periodic if it repeats values in regular intervals or periods.
### Formula
$$ f(x + P) = f(x) $$
where \( P \) is the period of the function.

## Reference Angles
### Description
A reference angle is the smallest angle between the terminal side of the given angle and the x-axis.
### Formula
For an angle \( \theta \):
- In quadrant I, the reference angle is \( \theta \).
- In quadrant II, the reference angle is \( 180^\circ - \theta \) or \( \pi - \theta \).
- In quadrant III, the reference angle is \( \theta - 180^\circ \) or \( \theta - \pi \).
- In quadrant IV, the reference angle is \( 360^\circ - \theta \) or \( 2\pi - \theta \).

## Trigonometric Functions of Any Angle
### Description
Trigonometric functions can be extended to any angle using their reference angles and signs in different quadrants.
### Formula
If \( \theta \) is an angle in standard position with terminal side in the given quadrant:
- Quadrant I: \( \sin(\theta) \), \( \cos(\theta) \), \( \tan(\theta) \) are positive.
- Quadrant II: \( \sin(\theta) \) is positive, \( \cos(\theta) \), \( \tan(\theta) \) are negative.
- Quadrant III: \( \tan(\theta) \) is positive, \( \sin(\theta) \), \( \cos(\theta) \) are negative.
- Quadrant IV: \( \cos(\theta) \) is positive, \( \sin(\theta) \), \( \tan(\theta) \) are negative.

## Pythagorean Identities
### Description
Pythagorean identities are derived from the Pythagorean theorem and relate the squares of the sine, cosine, and tangent functions.
### Formula
$$ \sin^2(\theta) + \cos^2(\theta) = 1 $$
$$ 1 + \tan^2(\theta) = \sec^2(\theta) $$
$$ 1 + \cot^2(\theta) = \csc^2(\theta) $$

## Cofunction Identities
### Description
Cofunction identities relate the trigonometric functions of complementary angles.
### Formula
$$ \sin(90^\circ - \theta) = \cos(\theta) $$
$$ \cos(90^\circ - \theta) = \sin(\theta) $$
$$ \tan(90^\circ - \theta) = \cot(\theta) $$
$$ \cot(90^\circ - \theta) = \tan(\theta) $$
$$ \sec(90^\circ - \theta) = \csc(\theta) $$
$$ \csc(90^\circ - \theta) = \sec(\theta) $$

## Even and Odd Functions
### Description
Trigonometric functions are classified as even or odd based on their symmetry properties.
### Formula
- Even functions: \( \cos(-\theta) = \cos(\theta) \), \( \sec(-\theta) = \sec(\theta) \)
- Odd functions: \( \sin(-\theta) = -\sin(\theta) \), \( \cos(-\theta) = -\cos(\theta) \), \( \tan(-\theta) = -\tan(\theta) \), \( \cot(-\theta) = -\cot(\theta) \), \( \csc(-\theta) = -\csc(\theta) \)
